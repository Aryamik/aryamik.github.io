Doomers are people who are extremely pessimistic or fatalistic about global problems such as overpopulation, peak oil, climate change, ecological overshoot, pollution, nuclear weapons, and runaway artificial intelligence. The term, and its associated term doomerism, arose primarily on social media. Some doomers believe these problems may lead to human extinction.

Let us look at some of the key headlines that may have fueled the doomerism movement:

  • Record heatwaves in Southeast Asia

I was curious to examine the root cause of what fuels doomerism. My immediate reaction was ‘social media’. And there is growing evidence to support this. Work by Jonathan Haidt does indicate that social media has played a major role in increased polarization we are currently witnessing.

But social media can’t be the cause of all evil, can it? Going back to the definition of doomerism, it is marked by having an extremely pessimistic or fatalistic view about the future; a feeling of dread, nihilism and anxiety. Yes, social media can exacerbate these feelings but the underlying idea behind doomerism is that no matter what we do; no matter how many EVs I drive, how many Solar Panels I install at my home, how many trees I plant - none of it matters in the grand scheme of things.

To put this into perspective, consider this statement I first came across in Kurzgesagt’s What Can You do to Stop Climate Change? And Should You?

If you eliminated 100% of your emissions for the rest of your life, you would save one second’s worth of emissions from the global energy sector.

This dwarves in comparison to the global emissions and even the most motivated person can’t make a tiny dent. One can feel like Sisyphus who is doomed to push the rock forever.

In The Meaning of Anxiety, Rollo May asserts that anxiety serves a purpose. Traditionally, the purpose was to protect us from primitive dangers. However, in the modern society, the purpose is to protect us from from dangers that threaten our existence or values that we identify with. In other words, anxiety is our human awareness of the fact that each of us is a being confronted with nonbeing. Nonbeing is that which would destroy being, such as death, severe illness, interpersonal hostility, too sudden change which destroys our psychological rootedness.

May concludes that we can temporarily alleviate our anxiety by shielding ourselves and ignoring the problem causing the anxiety. But that comes at a price of our human potential, our selfhood, our creativity. In other words we are stifling ourselves to alleviate this anxiety and therefore the positive aspects of selfhood develop as the individual confronts, moves through, and overcomes anxiety-creating experiences.

Kierkegaard describes this eloquently in The Sickness Unto Death:

So it is too that in the eyes of the world it is dangerous to venture. And why? Because one may lose. But not to venture is shrewd. And yet, by not venturing, it is so dreadfully easy to lose that which it would be difficult to lose in even the most venturesome venture, and in any case never so easily, so completely as if it were nothing—one’s self. For if I have ventured amiss—very well, then life helps me by its punishment. But if I have not ventured at all—who then helps me? And, moreover, if by not venturing at all in the highest sense (and to venture in the highest sense is precisely to become conscious of oneself) I have gained all earthly advantages . . . and lose my self! What of that?

No wonder Kierkegaard called anxiety a better teacher than reality, for while reality situations may be temporarily evaded, anxiety is an inner function which cannot be escaped short of constriction of the personality. It is a signal that something is wrong in one’s personality and one’s human relationships. It may be viewed as an inward cry for the resolution of the problem. However, it is this inward cry that has inspired us to create works of art, poetry, drama, science and other expressions of human civilization. It is the same inward cry that made these following events possible in 2024:

  • Per Capita CO2 Emissions Have Peaked Globally

  • Land set aside for nature is increasing.

  • Support for nuclear energy is increasing.

  • Many beloved animal species including sea turtles, Iberian lynx wildcat, and the red-cockaded woodpecker whose numbers were dwindling are making a comeback.

That’s the thing about doomerism - not only does it stifle our potential, it also makes us lose sight of the good stuff that is happening around us.

To wrap things up, the idea behind this post was not to say its all rainbows and sunshine. Yes, times are difficult and concerning. But it’s about cultivating a spirit of ‘mindful optimism’ that Max Tegmark and Erik Brynjolfsson talk about in Life 3.0. According to Erik, there are two kinds of optimism. First there’s the unconditional kind, such as the positive expectation that the Sun will rise tomorrow morning. Then there’s what he called “mindful optimism,” which is the expectation that good things will happen if you plan carefully and work hard for them.

My first encounter with ‘Mindful Optimists’ was in Parts Unknown where Anthony Bourdain went to areas like Provincetown (dealing with the aftermath of the opoid crisis), Puerto Rico (Hurricane Maria in 2017), Libya (decades of civil unrest and diplomatic tensions), Sri Lanka (aftermaths of the Sri Lankan Civil War), Detroit (economic fallout) and other areas that were desolate. One of the questions Bourdain asked the folks living in these areas was

Where do you see this place in X years?

Almost all the individuals living these areas had one trait in common - resilience. They had the attitude of rolling up their sleeves and going to work knowing very well that things are not looking good currently.

The ending monologue in the movie Arrival perfectly encapsulates this sentiment:

Despite knowing the journey and where it leads, I embrace it. And I welcome every moment of it.

Cultivating this mindful optimism is something I want to do in 2025 and I hope we get to see more of that!
